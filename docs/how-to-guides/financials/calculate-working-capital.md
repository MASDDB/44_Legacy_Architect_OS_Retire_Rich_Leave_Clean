# How to Calculate Working Capital

**Understand your capital requirements for business operations and sale preparation.**

*Time required: 30 minutes*

---

## What is Working Capital?

**Working Capital** = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

**Why it matters**:
- **Operational health**: Shows if you can pay bills
- **Buyer concern**: Negative working capital is a red flag
- **Sale negotiation**: Often adjusted at closing
- **Exit Readiness Score**: Impacts financial score

---

## Understanding the Components

### Current Assets

**Cash and Cash Equivalents**:
- Bank account balances
- Money market accounts
- Short-term investments (< 1 year)

**Accounts Receivable (AR)**:
- Money customers owe you
- Invoices not yet paid
- Should be collectible within 90 days

**Inventory**:
- Products for sale
- Raw materials
- Work in progress
- Finished goods

**Prepaid Expenses**:
- Prepaid rent
- Prepaid insurance
- Prepaid subscriptions
- Other advance payments

**Other Current Assets**:
- Short-term notes receivable
- Employee advances
- Tax refunds due

### Current Liabilities

**Accounts Payable (AP)**:
- Money you owe suppliers
- Unpaid vendor invoices
- Usually due within 30-60 days

**Accrued Expenses**:
- Wages payable
- Taxes payable
- Interest payable
- Utilities payable

**Current Portion of Long-Term Debt**:
- Loan payments due within 12 months
- Line of credit balance
- Credit card balances

**Deferred Revenue**:
- Customer prepayments
- Unearned revenue
- Advance deposits

**Other Current Liabilities**:
- Short-term notes payable
- Dividends payable
- Customer deposits

## Step 2: Enter Current Assets

### Cash

**Enter total cash**:
- All bank accounts
- Petty cash
- Cash equivalents

**Example**: $50,000

### Accounts Receivable

**Enter AR balance**:
- Total invoices outstanding
- Only include collectible amounts
- Exclude bad debts

**Example**: $75,000

**AR Aging** (optional but recommended):
- 0-30 days: $40,000
- 31-60 days: $25,000
- 61-90 days: $8,000
- 90+ days: $2,000 (may be uncollectible)

### Inventory

**Enter inventory value**:
- At cost (not retail price)
- Current market value
- Exclude obsolete inventory

**Example**: $30,000

**Inventory breakdown** (optional):
- Raw materials: $10,000
- Work in progress: $5,000
- Finished goods: $15,000

### Prepaid Expenses

**Enter prepaid amounts**:
- List each prepayment
- Amount and what it's for

**Example**: $5,000
- Prepaid insurance: $3,000
- Prepaid rent: $2,000

### Total Current Assets

**Auto-calculated**:
- Cash: $50,000
- AR: $75,000
- Inventory: $30,000
- Prepaid: $5,000
- **Total: $160,000**

## Step 4: Calculate Working Capital

### Basic Calculation

**Working Capital** = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

**Example**:
- Current Assets: $160,000
- Current Liabilities: $120,000
- **Working Capital: $40,000**

### Working Capital Ratio

**Formula**: Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

**Example**: $160,000 ÷ $120,000 = 1.33

**Interpretation**:
- **< 1.0**: Negative working capital (concerning)
- **1.0-1.5**: Adequate working capital
- **1.5-2.0**: Healthy working capital
- **> 2.0**: Excess working capital (may be inefficient)

**Your Result**: 1.33 = Adequate

## Step 5: Analyze Your Results

### Positive Working Capital

**What it means**:
- You can pay short-term obligations
- Business is operationally healthy
- Buyers see lower risk

**Example**: $40,000 positive working capital
- Good sign for buyers
- Shows financial stability

### Negative Working Capital

**What it means**:
- Current liabilities exceed current assets
- May struggle to pay bills
- Red flag for buyers

**Example**: -$20,000 working capital
- Need to address before sale
- Buyers will discount price or require fix

### Working Capital Trends

**Track over time**:
- Monthly or quarterly
- Look for trends
- Improving = good
- Declining = investigate

**Example**:
- Q1: $30,000
- Q2: $35,000
- Q3: $40,000
- **Trend**: Improving ✅

## Working Capital in M&A Transactions

### Why Buyers Care

**Operational continuity**:
- Ensures business can operate post-sale
- Covers day-to-day expenses
- Maintains customer service

**Risk assessment**:
- Negative WC = higher risk
- May require seller financing
- Could reduce purchase price

### Target Working Capital

**Buyers typically want**:
- Normalized working capital
- Based on historical average
- Adjusted for seasonality

**Calculation**:
- Average last 12 months
- Exclude one-time items
- Adjust for growth

**Example**:
- Jan-Dec average: $38,000
- Exclude: $5,000 one-time prepayment
- **Target WC: $33,000**

### Working Capital Adjustment

**At closing**:
- Actual WC measured
- Compared to target
- Purchase price adjusted

**Example**:
- Target WC: $33,000
- Actual WC: $40,000
- **Seller receives extra $7,000**

**Or**:
- Target WC: $33,000
- Actual WC: $28,000
- **Seller owes buyer $5,000**

## Improving Your Working Capital

### Increase Current Assets

**Collect AR faster**:
- Offer early payment discounts
- Improve collection processes
- Follow up on overdue invoices

**Reduce inventory**:
- Sell slow-moving items
- Improve inventory turnover
- Just-in-time ordering

**Increase cash**:
- Improve profitability
- Reduce owner distributions
- Secure line of credit

### Decrease Current Liabilities

**Pay down debt**:
- Pay off credit cards
- Reduce line of credit
- Refinance short-term to long-term

**Negotiate terms**:
- Extend payment terms with vendors
- Negotiate better rates
- Consolidate payables

**Manage accruals**:
- Pay taxes on time
- Stay current on wages
- Avoid penalties

## Common Issues and Solutions

### Issue #1: High AR Balance

**Problem**: Customers slow to pay

**Solutions**:
- Tighten credit terms
- Require deposits
- Offer payment plans
- Use factoring (if desperate)

### Issue #2: Excess Inventory

**Problem**: Too much cash tied up in inventory

**Solutions**:
- Run clearance sales
- Improve forecasting
- Reduce order quantities
- Implement JIT inventory

### Issue #3: High AP Balance

**Problem**: Behind on vendor payments

**Solutions**:
- Negotiate payment plans
- Prioritize critical vendors
- Improve cash flow
- Consider short-term financing

### Issue #4: Seasonal Fluctuations

**Problem**: Working capital varies by season

**Solutions**:
- Calculate seasonal average
- Explain to buyers
- Show historical pattern
- Plan for seasonal needs

## Best Practices

### Track Monthly

**Regular monitoring**:
- Calculate every month
- Track trends
- Identify issues early
- Take corrective action

**Set up alerts**:
- If WC drops below threshold
- If ratio falls below 1.2
- If AR aging worsens

### Maintain Healthy Ratios

**Target ratios**:
- Working Capital Ratio: 1.3-1.8
- Quick Ratio: 1.0-1.5
- Current Ratio: 1.5-2.0

**Why**: Shows financial health to buyers

### Document Calculations

**For buyers**:
- Show your work
- Explain adjustments
- Provide historical data
- Be transparent

**Include**:
- Balance sheet support
- AR/AP aging reports
- Inventory valuations
- Debt schedules

### Plan for Target WC

**Before sale**:
- Calculate target WC
- Understand adjustment mechanism
- Plan to meet target
- Avoid surprises at closing

## Troubleshooting

### Problem: Negative working capital

**Solutions**:
1. **Inject capital**: Owner contribution
2. **Improve collections**: Get AR down
3. **Pay down debt**: Reduce current liabilities
4. **Increase profitability**: Generate more cash

### Problem: Working capital too high

**Solutions**:
1. **Not really a problem**: Shows strength
2. **May be inefficient**: Could deploy cash better
3. **Buyer may want lower target**: Negotiate

### Problem: Can't calculate accurately

**Solutions**:
1. **Get help**: Accountant or CFO
2. **Use balance sheet**: Pull from accounting system
3. **Estimate**: Better than nothing
4. **Improve over time**: Refine calculations
