# How to Document EBITDA Add-Backs

**Maximize your business valuation with properly documented add-backs.**

*Time required: 30-60 minutes*

---

## What Are EBITDA Add-Backs?

**EBITDA** = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization

**Add-backs** are expenses that:
- Appear on your P&L
- Reduce reported profit
- But won't continue after the sale
- Should be "added back" to show true earning power

**Why they matter**: Each dollar of add-back increases EBITDA, which increases your business value (typically by 3-10x the add-back amount).

---

## Common Legitimate Add-Backs

### Owner Compensation Above Market

**What it is**: You pay yourself more than market rate for your role

**Example**:
- You pay yourself $250K as CEO
- Market rate for hired CEO: $150K
- Add-back: $100K

**How to document**:
- Your current compensation (W-2, K-1, distributions)
- Market rate research (salary surveys, job postings)
- Calculation showing difference

### Owner Perks & Benefits

**What qualifies**:
- Personal vehicle expenses
- Personal travel disguised as business
- Country club memberships (if not business-necessary)
- Personal insurance paid by business
- Family members on payroll (if above market or not working)

**Example**:
- Company car (personal use): $15K/year
- Personal travel: $10K/year
- Spouse salary (minimal work): $40K/year
- Total add-back: $65K

**How to document**:
- List each expense with amount
- Explain why it's personal
- Show it won't continue under new owner

### One-Time Expenses

**What qualifies**:
- Legal fees for one-time issues
- Consultant fees for special projects
- Moving/relocation costs
- Lawsuit settlements
- Major repairs (not recurring maintenance)

**Example**:
- Legal fees for contract dispute: $50K (one-time)
- Office relocation: $30K (one-time)
- Total add-back: $80K

**How to document**:
- Invoice or receipt
- Explanation of why one-time
- Confirmation it won't recur

### Non-Recurring Events

**What qualifies**:
- Bad debt from customer bankruptcy
- Inventory write-offs (if unusual)
- Equipment write-downs
- Restructuring costs

**Example**:
- Customer bankruptcy bad debt: $75K
- One-time inventory obsolescence: $25K
- Total add-back: $100K

**How to document**:
- Specific event description
- Financial impact
- Why it won't recur

### Pre-Sale Preparation Costs

**What qualifies**:
- M&A advisor fees
- Legal fees for sale preparation
- Accounting fees for quality of earnings
- Data room preparation costs

**Example**:
- M&A advisor retainer: $25K
- Legal fees: $15K
- QofE report: $20K
- Total add-back: $60K

**How to document**:
- Invoices for services
- Explanation of purpose
- Confirmation these are sale-related

## Questionable Add-Backs (Use Caution)

### Rent to Related Party

**The claim**: "I rent my building to my business above market rate"

**Buyer concern**: Is the rent really above market? Will new owner get same deal?

**How to make it work**:
- Get independent appraisal of market rent
- Document the difference
- Ensure lease is transferable or building is included in sale

### Owner's Salary (Full Amount)

**The claim**: "Add back my entire $200K salary"

**Buyer concern**: Someone needs to run the business

**How to make it work**:
- Only add back amount above market rate
- Document market rate for your role
- Show business can run without you (or with cheaper replacement)

### "Discretionary" Expenses

**The claim**: "This marketing spend is discretionary"

**Buyer concern**: If we cut it, will revenue drop?

**How to make it work**:
- Show expense didn't drive revenue
- Prove it was wasteful or ineffective
- Demonstrate revenue maintained without it

## How to Document Add-Backs in Legacy Architect OS

### Step 1: Navigate to Financials

1. Log in to Legacy Architect OS
2. Click **"Financials"** in left sidebar
3. Click **"EBITDA Add-Backs"** tab

### Step 2: Add New Add-Back

1. Click **"Add New Add-Back"** button
2. Fill in the form:

**Add-Back Category** (select from dropdown):
- Owner Compensation
- Owner Perks
- One-Time Expense
- Non-Recurring Event
- Pre-Sale Costs
- Other

**Description**:
- Clear, specific description
- Example: "Owner salary above market rate"

**Amount**:
- Dollar amount to add back
- Example: $100,000

**Year/Period**:
- Which year does this apply to?
- Example: 2024

**Frequency**:
- One-time
- Annual
- Monthly (if ongoing)

### Step 3: Add Supporting Documentation

**Upload evidence**:
- Click **"Add Supporting Document"**
- Upload relevant files:
  - Invoices
  - Receipts
  - Salary surveys
  - Appraisals
  - Calculations

**Write justification**:
- Explain why this is a legitimate add-back
- Show your math
- Address potential buyer concerns

**Example justification**:
> "Owner (CEO) compensation of $250K is $100K above market rate for hired CEO in our industry and region. Market rate research from Salary.com and Glassdoor shows CEO salary range of $120K-$180K for companies our size. A new owner would likely hire a CEO at $150K, creating a $100K annual add-back."

### Step 4: Calculate Total EBITDA

The system automatically:
- Sums all add-backs
- Adds to reported EBITDA
- Shows adjusted EBITDA

**Example**:
- Reported EBITDA: $400K
- Total add-backs: $250K
- Adjusted EBITDA: $650K

### Step 5: Review and Refine

1. Review all add-backs for legitimacy
2. Remove questionable items
3. Strengthen documentation for each
4. Get advisor review (recommended)

## Best Practices

### Be Conservative

**Better to**:
- Understate add-backs slightly
- Only include rock-solid items
- Have bulletproof documentation

**Than to**:
- Overstate add-backs
- Include questionable items
- Have weak documentation

**Why**: Buyers will scrutinize and discount questionable add-backs. Better to have $200K of solid add-backs than $300K of questionable ones.

### Document Everything

For each add-back, have:
1. **The expense** (invoice, receipt, P&L line item)
2. **The justification** (why it's an add-back)
3. **The evidence** (market data, appraisals, etc.)

### Get Professional Help

Consider hiring:
- **CPA** - To review and validate add-backs
- **M&A Advisor** - To assess buyer acceptability
- **Business Appraiser** - For formal valuation

**Cost**: $2K-$10K  
**Benefit**: Could increase sale price by $100K-$500K+

### Update Annually

- Review add-backs each year
- Add new ones as they occur
- Remove ones that no longer apply
- Keep documentation current

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake #1: No Documentation

**Problem**: "Trust me, this is an add-back"

**Solution**: Document everything with evidence

### Mistake #2: Overly Aggressive

**Problem**: Adding back normal business expenses

**Solution**: Only add back truly non-recurring or owner-specific items

### Mistake #3: Inconsistent Over Time

**Problem**: Different add-backs each year with no explanation

**Solution**: Be consistent or explain changes

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Buyer Perspective

**Problem**: "I think this is an add-back"

**Solution**: Ask "Would a buyer accept this?"

### Mistake #5: Poor Record-Keeping

**Problem**: Can't find supporting documents during due diligence

**Solution**: Organize and upload all documentation now

## Example: Complete Add-Back Documentation

### Add-Back: Owner Salary Above Market

**Amount**: $100,000

**Category**: Owner Compensation

**Year**: 2024

**Description**:
> Owner/CEO total compensation of $250,000 exceeds market rate for hired CEO by approximately $100,000.

**Supporting Documentation**:
1. **Owner W-2** showing $250K compensation
2. **Salary Survey** from Salary.com for CEO role
   - Company size: $5M-$10M revenue
   - Industry: Professional Services
   - Location: Chicago metro
   - Range: $120K-$180K
   - Median: $150K
3. **Job Postings** for similar CEO roles ($140K-$165K)
4. **Calculation**:
   - Owner compensation: $250K
   - Market rate (median): $150K
   - Add-back: $100K

**Justification**:
> A new owner would likely hire a professional CEO at market rate ($150K) rather than pay the current owner's compensation ($250K). This creates a legitimate $100K annual add-back that increases the business's true earning power.

## Valuation Impact

### The Multiplier Effect

**Example**:
- Adjusted EBITDA add-backs: $250K
- Industry multiple: 5x
- Valuation increase: $1.25M

**Calculation**:
- Without add-backs: $400K EBITDA × 5 = $2.0M value
- With add-backs: $650K EBITDA × 5 = $3.25M value
- **Difference: $1.25M**

### Why Buyers Accept Add-Backs

Buyers understand:
- Owners often run expenses through business
- Some expenses are truly one-time
- Adjusted EBITDA shows true earning power

**But they require**:
- Solid documentation
- Conservative estimates
- Professional validation
