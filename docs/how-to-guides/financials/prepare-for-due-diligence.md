# How to Prepare for Due Diligence

**Organize and audit your financial records to survive buyer scrutiny and close the deal.**

*Time required: 2-3 hours (initial setup)*

---

## What is Financial Due Diligence?

**Financial Due Diligence** is the process where a buyer verifies your financial information to ensure the business is performing as represented.

**Why it matters**:
- **Validates value**: Confirms the purchase price is justified
- **Uncovers risks**: Identifies tax liabilities, bad debts, or declining trends
- **Deal killer**: Poor record-keeping is a top reason deals fall apart
- **Speed to close**: Organized records typically lead to a faster closing

---

## checklist: The "Must-Have" Documents

Before starting, ensure you have access to these core documents for the last **3-5 years**:

- [ ] Profit & Loss Statements (Annual)
- [ ] Balance Sheets (Annual)
- [ ] Cash Flow Statements (Annual)
- [ ] Tax Returns (Federal & State)
- [ ] Monthly Management Reports (Trailing 24 months)
- [ ] Bank Statements
- [ ] Accounts Receivable/Payable Aging Reports

---

## Step 1: Organize Annual Financial Statements

Buyers need to see a consistent history of performance.

1. **Gather Annual Reports**:
   - Years: Current year-to-date (YTD) + previous 3 full years
   - Formats: PDF (signed) and Excel (if requested)
   - Basis: Accrual basis is preferred by most buyers; typically required for deals >$2M.

2. **Verify Consistency**:
   - Do the numbers match your tax returns? (If not, have a reconciliation ready)
   - Are categories consistent year-over-year?

3. **Upload to Data Room**:
   - Navigation: **Data Room** > **Financials** > **Annual Statements**
   - Naming convention: `YYYY_Annual_Financials.pdf`

---

## Step 2: Prepare Monthly Management Reports

Buyers analyze monthly trends to spot seasonality, growth spurts, or recent declines.

1. **Generate Monthly P&L**:
   - Range: Last 24 months minimum
   - View: Month-by-month comparison columns

2. **Analyze Trends**:
   - Check for unusual spikes or drops
   - **Tip**: Add notes explaining anomalies (e.g., "Feb 2024 dip due to supply chain delay")

3. **Upload to Data Room**:
   - Navigation: **Data Room** > **Financials** > **Monthly Reports**

---

## Step 3: Document Revenue & Customer Data

Prove the quality of your revenue.

1. **Revenue by Customer**:
   - Report: Sales by Customer Summary
   - Risk check: Does any single customer represent >10-20% of revenue? (Customer Concentration)
   
2. **Revenue by Product/Service**:
   - Report: Sales by Item/Service
   - Purpose: Shows what drives profit margins

3. **Recurring Revenue Proof** (if applicable):
   - Churn rates
   - Contract files
   - Renewal history

---

## Step 4: Audit Assets & Liabilities

Verify that what you own and what you owe are accurately recorded.

### Assets
- **Inventory**: perform a physical count. Remove obsolete items from the valuation.
- **Accounts Receivable**: Review aging report. Write off uncollectible bad debt.
- **Fixed Assets**: Ensure asset list matches reality (computers, vehicles, machinery).

### Liabilities
- **Accounts Payable**: Ensure all vendor bills are entered.
- **Loans**: Have loan agreements and current amortization schedules ready.
- **Contingent Liabilities**: Disclose potential lawsuits or warranty claims.

---

## Step 5: Prepare Quality of Earnings (Q of E) Support

Sophisticated buyers (especially PE firms) will focus on "Adjusted EBITDA."

1. **Owner Compensation**:
   - Document your salary vs. market rate for a replacement manager.
   
2. **EBITDA Add-Backs**:
   - Personal expenses (cars, travel, meals)
   - One-time legal fees
   - Non-recurring repairs
   - *Refer to [Document EBITDA Add-Backs](document-ebitda-addbacks.md) for details.*

3. **Working Capital**:
   - Calculate average monthly working capital.
   - *Refer to [Calculate Working Capital](calculate-working-capital.md) for details.*

## Common Red Flags & Solutions

### 1. Financials don't match Tax Returns
- **Issue**: P&L shows $500k profit, Tax Return shows $200k.
- **Solution**: Create a **Book-to-Tax Reconciliation** document explaining the differences (e.g., depreciation methods, non-deductible expenses).

### 2. Comingling Personal Expenses
- **Issue**: Personal vacactions or vehicles on the books.
- **Solution**: Clearly identify these as "Discretionary Earnings" add-backs. Stop this practice immediately for the current year.

### 3. Poor Inventory Records
- **Issue**: Inventory value is an estimate or guess.
- **Solution**: Conduct a full physical inventory count immediately before going to market.

### 4. "Cash Basis" Accounting for larger deals
- **Issue**: Recognizes revenue when cash hits bank, not when earned.
- **Solution**: If deal size >$2-5M, spend the money to convert to **Accrual Basis** (GAAP) financials.

## Best Practices

### Pre-Diligence Audit
- Hire a CPA to do a "dummy" due diligence audit on your own firm *before* a buyer does.
- Fix issues they find so the buyer sees a clean house.

### The "Data Room" Rule
- Never send files via email.
- Use the **Data Room** module in Legacy Architect OS to maintain control and see who views what.

### Be Honest About "Warts"
- **Disclose early**: If you lost a big customer or have a lawsuit, tell the buyer *before* they find it.
- **Control the narrative**: "We lost Client X, but here is our plan that has already replaced 50% of that revenue."
